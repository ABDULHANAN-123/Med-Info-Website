ASPIRIN Aspirin, also known as acetylsalicylic acid, is a widely used medication with various applications, particularly as an anti-inflammatory and pain reliever. It is available in tablet form, often taken orally. Below is a detailed overview of aspirin tablets: Chemical Composition Active Ingredient: Acetylsalicylic acid. Inactive Ingredients (may vary by manufacturer): Cornstarch, lactose, microcrystalline cellulose, and others. Uses of Aspirin Pain Relief: Effective for mild to moderate pain such as headaches, menstrual cramps, muscle aches, and toothaches. Anti-inflammatory: Used in conditions like arthritis to reduce swelling and inflammation. Fever Reduction: Acts as an antipyretic to lower fever. Cardiovascular Health: Low-dose aspirin (81 mg): Helps prevent blood clots, reducing the risk of heart attack and stroke in individuals with cardiovascular disease. Prescribed for patients after certain heart procedures like angioplasty. Prevention of Blood Clots: Sometimes recommended in conditions like atrial fibrillation (under medical supervision). Dosage Forms Low-dose tablets: Typically 75-100 mg (commonly 81 mg, referred to as "baby aspirin"). Regular-dose tablets: 325 mg. High-dose tablets: 500 mg or more, generally for acute pain or inflammation. Mechanism of Action Aspirin works by inhibiting cyclooxygenase (COX) enzymes, specifically COX-1 and COX-2. This prevents the formation of prostaglandins, which are chemicals responsible for pain, fever, and inflammation. It also inhibits thromboxane, reducing platelet aggregation and preventing blood clots. Administration Route: Oral (tablet taken with water). Dosage: Depends on the condition being treated. For example: Pain relief: 325-650 mg every 4-6 hours as needed. Cardiovascular prevention: 75-100 mg daily. Side Effects Common Side Effects: Nausea or vomiting. Heartburn or upset stomach. Serious Side Effects (less common): Stomach ulcers or bleeding. Allergic reactions (rash, difficulty breathing). Ringing in the ears (tinnitus), especially at higher doses. Liver or kidney damage with prolonged use. Precautions Avoid in Specific Conditions: Bleeding disorders (e.g., hemophilia). Stomach ulcers or a history of gastrointestinal bleeding. Allergies to aspirin or NSAIDs. Contraindications: Children under 16 with viral infections (risk of Reye's syndrome). Pregnancy (especially in the third trimester unless prescribed by a doctor). Drug Interactions: Can interact with blood thinners (e.g., warfarin), other NSAIDs, or certain diuretics. Storage Store in a cool, dry place. Keep out of reach of children. Discard if the tablets smell like vinegar (indicates degradation). Key Considerations Aspirin should always be taken as directed by a healthcare provider, especially in cases of long-term use for heart health. Overdose can cause toxicity, leading to symptoms like confusion, hyperventilation, and even coma. Aspirin is a versatile medication but requires careful use to balance its benefits and risks effectively.
